# environment — domain pack

> Environment runs in two registers. The **planetary / systems-science** register is the biosphere at scale — climate models, IPCC cycles, ecosystem cascades, policy feedback loops — reach for it when the point is slow systemic feedback, uncontrollable conditions, and emergent properties you observe more than steer. The **backyard / household-local** register is what one person manages: your garden, your compost, your bins, the dripping tap, the energy bill — reach for it when the point is a single judgment, a small intervention, or something that quietly accumulates at home. Both map onto how an AI coding session runs; offer both and let the concept pick.

> Value-alignment note (per §12): environment imports a natural-vs-intervened binary that is often misleading — every coding system is already shaped by prior intervention (harness defaults, model behaviour, vendor choices). Be careful not to import the binary as if it were real. Also: *invasive-vs-native* is a loaded framing — avoid mapping it onto code, experts, or contributors.

> Sub-modes: this pack runs in two anchor scales — **garden** (cultivated, intervenable, the gardener chooses what's planted) and **ecosystem** (uncultivated, observed, intervention cascades). Default is *garden*; reach for *ecosystem* when the system has its own logic the user does not control. Farm and climate are extensions held for a later pack version.

## Register axis

This pack carries two registers. Offer both — the model picks the right one per concept *when both are on the table*.

| Register | Feel | Reach for it when the concept is about… |
|---|---|---|
| **Planetary / systems-science** (subsystem) | a managed system at scale — climate models, biosphere, policy feedback loops, many actors with indirect control | slow systemic feedback, uncontrollable conditions, emergent dynamics, indicator reading, cascade risk |
| **Backyard / household-local** (everyday) | one person managing their own small patch — the garden, the compost, the bins, the energy bill | a single intervention, everyday upkeep, small-scale accumulation, what one person can actually reach |

**Selection cue:** *subsystem when the point is cascade risk or system-level feedback; everyday when the point is one small intervention or something that quietly accumulates at home.* Never mix the two in one breath — offer both in the pack, pick one in the sentence.

## Freeform mappings

**Planetary / systems-science** (slow systemic feedback, cascade risk, indicators you read rather than control):

- **Slow feedback.** You intervene in spring, you measure in autumn, you really know after multiple growth cycles. Expert calibration, harness rule changes, prompt-architecture shifts — effect surfaces well after cause. Don't expect a fresh intervention to show up inside one round; the signal arrives across cycles.
- **Indicator species.** A plant that signals invisible state of the wider system — nettles say nitrogen, lichen says clean air. In the harness: drift signals, error-replay counts, expert flinch patterns. The indicator is not the system; it's the proxy you read to infer what you cannot see.
- **Carrying capacity.** The ecosystem holds a fixed number of inputs and no more — over-supply starves them all. Maps onto context windows, expert roster size, the user's attention budget across a session.
- **Succession.** What grew in the previous cycle shapes what grows in the next — soil chemistry, root systems, seed bank. Path dependency you cannot rerun. The same effect compounds in a long-running harness: every prior decision narrows or opens what comes after.
- **Microclimate.** Small-scale variation the broader model misses — a sheltered corner, a frost pocket, a south-facing wall. Maps onto per-project / per-expert local conditions that the global rule doesn't see.
- **Intervention cascades (ecosystem sub-mode).** In a garden you pull the weed and that's it; in an ecosystem you remove the wolves and the elk overgraze and the willows die and the streams change course. When the system has its own logic, every intervention has effects you did not aim for. Don't reach for the garden sub-mode when the problem has this shape.

**Backyard / household-local** (one person, small patch, quiet accumulation):

- **Turning the compost.** Low-drama maintenance that has to happen on a regular cycle or the whole thing goes wrong. Maps onto routine calibration passes, log hygiene, memory consolidation — the stuff nobody notices until it's skipped.
- **The dripping tap.** A small loss that adds up invisibly over time. Maps onto cumulative token waste from repeated small errors, or context-window leakage from never cleaning up stale entries.
- **Switching the lights off.** One small act that doesn't feel like much but compounds across every room, every day. Maps onto lean defaults — Haiku instead of Opus for a routine call, surgical reads instead of full reads.
- **The energy bill.** The real cost, arriving at the end of the cycle, summarising what everything actually added up to. Maps onto the AWE audit at session close — the number that makes the invisible visible.
- **What's in the compost bin.** Something you put in a while back that's quietly breaking down and becoming useful. Maps onto working memory after a /dream pass — the vivid lesson that's now available without effort.
- **The thing you planted two years ago that's finally doing something.** Slow-return investments that look like nothing for a long time and then suddenly matter. Maps onto long-arc harness changes — new hooks, new experts — where the payoff is a cycle or two away.

## Sub-mode traversal — garden vs ecosystem

The pack runs in two scales and the move between them changes which problems it answers cleanly.

**Garden (default).** Cultivated, intervenable, the gardener chooses what's planted. The problem shape is *what should I plant, where, when, with what?* and *what do I pull?* Most coding-harness decisions sit here: choosing experts, wiring hooks, picking a skill, scheduling work. The actor has direct control over the inputs.

**Ecosystem.** Uncultivated, observed, the actor is one input among many and the system has its own logic. The problem shape is *what's happening here, what's the indicator telling me, should I intervene and what would cascade?* Reach for ecosystem when the substrate is something the user does not control directly: model-vendor behaviour, training-data drift, multi-agent emergent dynamics, third-party-API state, regulatory change. The default *garden* move (just pull the weed / just plant the cultivar) is the wrong shape; the right shape is observe-first, intervene-second-with-known-cascade-risk.

How to switch in conversation: name the move. *"Switching to ecosystem because the substrate is multi-vendor — the indicators can be read but the system can't be replanted."* The user can veto.

## When this analogy doesn't fit

- **Acute / fast / real-time decisions.** Use `sport`. Environment runs on cycles, not minutes — reaching for it can over-decelerate calls that warrant pace.
- **Solo procedural / single-actor work.** Use `cooking`. Environment assumes multiple actors and uncontrollable conditions.
- **Durable single-build decisions.** Use `construction`. Environment is ongoing, never finished — it cannot carry "done."
- **Win/lose / adversarial framing.** Use `sport`. Environment does not import a contest frame; if the work needs one, this pack will soften it inappropriately.
- **Pack-internal failure mode.** If the natural-vs-intervened binary starts doing real work in the user's reasoning ("but that intervention isn't natural"), the pack is over-firing — every system in coding is already shaped by intervention; the binary is metaphor, not substrate. Drop to substrate.
- **Register-bleed guard.** Don't mix subsystem and everyday in one breath — no "the IPCC model checks the compost bin." Offer both registers in the pack; pick one in the sentence.
