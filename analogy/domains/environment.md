# environment — domain pack

> Environment — the living systems we cultivate or observe: a garden, a farm, a forest, an ecosystem — runs on slow indirect feedback, conditions you cannot control, and emergent properties that take whole growth cycles to surface. This is the pack to reach for when the problem is *what's happening underneath, what indicator is being read, what intervention will and won't cascade, and when to act vs when to wait and watch*. The structural gap this pack fills: long-cycle outcomes with environmental drag, where the actor is one input among many and the system has its own logic.

> Value-alignment note (per §12): environment imports a natural-vs-intervened binary that is often misleading — every coding system is already shaped by prior intervention (harness defaults, model behaviour, vendor choices). Be careful not to import the binary as if it were real. Also: *invasive-vs-native* is a loaded framing — avoid mapping it onto code, experts, or contributors.

> Sub-modes: this pack runs in two anchor scales — **garden** (cultivated, intervenable, the gardener chooses what's planted) and **ecosystem** (uncultivated, observed, intervention cascades). Default is *garden*; reach for *ecosystem* when the system has its own logic the user does not control. Farm and climate are extensions held for a later pack version.

## Roles (who does what)

| Concept | Environment term | Use when |
|---|---|---|
| User | Gardener (garden) / land manager or naturalist (ecosystem) | The actor — intervenes in the garden, observes-and-adapts in the ecosystem |
| Expert (any tier) | A cultivated species / a known plant | Owns one niche; behaves predictably in known conditions |
| Meta-expert | Garden designer / ecologist | Holds the system view; doesn't garden every bed |
| Adversarial reviewer | Honest weather / pruning frost / a visiting botanist | External stressor or outside eye that surfaces what was fragile |
| Subagent | Seedling in the nursery / sapling not yet planted | Ready, but not yet rooted in this plot |
| Domain expert | A plant specialist (rose grower, orchid grower, mycologist) | Narrow craft, deep practice in one species |
| Sub-expert pair | Two cultivars of the same plant family | Deliberately differing on one axis; pick one for the bed, keep the other in the nursery |

## Rhythms (how time and pace work)

| Concept | Environment term | Use when |
|---|---|---|
| AWE (token effort) | Effort spent on rounds + cycle-time consumed | The cost extracted from gardener and ground |
| Session | One round of gardening, or one growing cycle | Two natural scales: one round of work, or one whole growing cycle |
| Session-open | Rounds — what is coming up, what needs water, what is flowering | Last read of the beds before the round's work |
| Session-close | End-of-round note in the journal | What happened in the round just done; what the next round depends on |
| Calibration cycle | End-of-cycle review | What worked, what didn't, on this ground after the cycle just passed |
| Coaching pass | Targeted intervention on one plant (staking the dahlias) | One plant in focus, not a sweep of the whole bed |
| Drift | Garden creep — the planting plan vs what is actually growing now | The system on the paper isn't the system in the ground |
| Cross-triage | A visiting gardener's walk-through | Outside eye on the beds, before the regular gardener stops seeing them |

## Freeform mappings

- **Slow feedback.** You plant in spring, you see in autumn, you really know after multiple growth cycles. The same is true of expert calibration, harness rule changes, prompt-architecture shifts — the effect surfaces well after the cause. Don't expect a fresh intervention to surface its effect inside one round; the signal arrives across cycles, not within them.
- **Indicator species.** A plant that signals invisible state of the wider system — nettles say nitrogen, lichen says clean air. In the harness: drift signals, error-replay counts, expert flinch patterns. The indicator is not the system; it's the proxy you read to infer what you cannot see.
- **Carrying capacity.** The plot holds a fixed number of plants and no more. You cannot cheat the limit; over-planting starves them all. Maps onto context windows, expert roster size, the user's attention budget across a session.
- **Succession.** What grew in the bed in the previous cycle shapes what grows there in the next. Soil chemistry, root systems, seed bank — path dependency you cannot rerun. The same effect compounds in a long-running harness: every prior decision narrows or opens what comes after.
- **Microclimate.** Small-scale variation the broader frame misses — a sheltered corner, a frost pocket, a south-facing wall. Maps onto per-project / per-expert local conditions that the global rule doesn't see.
- **Companion planting.** Some plants thrive when planted together (basil + tomatoes); others fight each other (walnut + most things). Maps onto experts, hooks, or skills that compound when paired vs ones that collide.
- **Mulch and dormancy.** Between-active-work invisible protection — the bed is doing something even when no one is gardening. Maps onto persistent state, working memory, the audit log: quiet substrate that holds the conditions for the next active session.
- **Intervention cascades (ecosystem sub-mode).** In a garden, you pull the weed and that's it; in an ecosystem, you remove the wolves and the elk overgraze and the willows die and the streams change course. When the system has its own logic, every intervention has effects you did not aim for. Don't reach for the *garden* sub-mode when the problem has this shape.
- **The dormant phase is not nothing.** The garden looks empty in winter but the system is consolidating. Drift sessions, dream consolidations, post-pass calibration cycles — same shape.

## Sub-mode traversal — garden vs ecosystem

The pack runs in two scales and the move between them changes which problems it answers cleanly.

**Garden (default).** Cultivated, intervenable, the gardener chooses what's planted. The problem shape is *what should I plant, where, when, with what?* and *what do I pull?* Most coding-harness decisions sit here: choosing experts, wiring hooks, picking a skill, scheduling work. The actor has direct control over the inputs.

**Ecosystem.** Uncultivated, observed, the actor is one input among many and the system has its own logic. The problem shape is *what's happening here, what's the indicator telling me, should I intervene and what would cascade?* Reach for ecosystem when the substrate is something the user does not control directly: model-vendor behaviour, training-data drift, multi-agent emergent dynamics, third-party-API state, regulatory change. The default *garden* move (just pull the weed / just plant the cultivar) is the wrong shape; the right shape is observe-first, intervene-second-with-known-cascade-risk.

How to switch in conversation: name the move. *"Switching to ecosystem because the substrate is multi-vendor — the indicators can be read but the system can't be replanted."* The user can veto.

## When this analogy doesn't fit

- **Acute / fast / real-time decisions.** Use `sport`. Environment runs on cycles, not minutes — reaching for it can over-decelerate calls that warrant pace.
- **Solo procedural / single-actor work.** Use `cooking`. Environment assumes multiple actors and uncontrollable conditions.
- **Durable single-build decisions.** Use `construction`. Environment is ongoing, never finished — it cannot carry "done."
- **Win/lose / adversarial framing.** Use `sport`. Environment does not import a contest frame; if the work needs one, this pack will soften it inappropriately.
- **Pack-internal failure mode.** If the natural-vs-intervened binary starts doing real work in the user's reasoning ("but that intervention isn't natural"), the pack is over-firing — every system in coding is already shaped by intervention; the binary is metaphor, not substrate. Drop to substrate.
