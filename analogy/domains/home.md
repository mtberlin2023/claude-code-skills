# home — domain pack

> Home is where decisions accumulate. Most are individually small; the bill at the end of the cycle is the cumulative truth. This is the pack to reach for when the problem is *a series of small choices that nobody has audited as a whole* — the §10 subscription-audit register from the paper, or any cumulative-budget shape.

---

## Register note — home is single-register by design

Most packs in this set carry two registers (a professional **subsystem** + an **everyday** one). **Home is the deliberate exception: it *is* the everyday register.** Its whole value is one household's accumulating, mostly-unaudited decisions — the everyday is the point, not one half of an axis.

Coordination still happens — someone has to keep the calendar, run the rota, chase the bills — but in `home` those jobs are played by **ordinary household members**, not a hired professional tier. There is no estate manager and no staff; there's just the person who happens to organise everyone. When the problem genuinely needs a professional-coordination register (formal contracts, a managed build, a crew), reach for **construction** — that's its job.

## Roles (who does what)

| Concept | Home term | Use when |
|---|---|---|
| User | Head of household | The one who has to actually live with the call |
| Expert (any tier) | A trade you call in (plumber, electrician, accountant) | One narrow job; doesn't run the house |
| Meta-expert | Interior architect / financial advisor — or whoever in the house keeps the master plan | Sets layout / structure; doesn't live with the day-to-day |
| Adversarial reviewer | House survey / energy assessor / nosy neighbour | Outside eyes that catch what habit normalised |
| Subagent | Cleaner / babysitter / part-time help | Owns one chore window; doesn't decide |
| Domain expert | Specialist (lawyer for a will, surveyor for a survey) | Called for one specific question |
| Sub-expert pair | Two opinions on the same renovation | Deliberately differing views on the same call |

## Freeform mappings

- **The kitchen table** — where the real decisions get made. Not the spreadsheet, not the inbox.
- **The family member who runs the admin** — the one who keeps the calendar, chases the bills, and remembers the renewals. Coordination doesn't need a hired manager; someone in the house already plays it. This is `home`'s answer to "who co-ordinates?" — an ordinary person, not an estate manager.
- **The chore rota** — whose turn for what, made visible. The everyday version of a duty roster.
- **Standing orders / direct debits** — recurring commitments that quietly compound. The subscription-audit problem lives here.
- **The bills drawer** — where the open questions pile up until someone has the energy.
- **A free trial that quietly converted** — the paradigmatic accumulated-without-noticing item.
- **Spring cleaning** — periodic audit; the household calibration cycle made visible.
- **The junk drawer** — accumulated low-stakes items nobody has time to sort. Maps onto every backlog.
- **The cupboard at the back of the kitchen** — what's still there but nobody opens.
- **Routines vs novelties** — recurring expenses vs one-offs; budgets fail when novelties become routines silently.
- **The household budget** — the cumulative truth; nothing in it lies on its own, but the sum often does.
- **"Who's doing the school run?"** — capacity assignment under load. Everyone's role brief, re-decided each cycle.

## When this analogy doesn't fit

- **Single high-stakes architectural calls.** Use construction. Home assumes things will continue, not be poured once.
- **Tight technical workflows.** Cooking is faster and more precise. Home is too soft for service-speed problems.
- **Professional coordination — contracts, a crew, a managed build.** Use construction. Home's coordination is ordinary household members, not a hired tier; if you need the professional register, that's construction's job.
- **Adversarial / contractual disputes.** Home imports a collaborative frame that will hide real opposition.
- **Acute-cost decisions where speed beats cumulative care.** Home is built for slow accumulation, not crisis response.
